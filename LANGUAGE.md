# LANGUAGE.md — The voice doctrine

The canonical home for *how the game talks*. Tone, register, person, tense, Greek diction,
typography, punctuation, and the locked decisions that came out of playtest. Consolidates
the older treatments in `PHASE-ARCHITECTURE.md §6` (Tone doctrine) and
`design-system/README.md` (CONTENT FUNDAMENTALS) — those now point here.

If you are about to write a single new line in the game, read this first.

---

## 1. The thesis

**Comic‑heightened with an earned ache, often leaning all the way into parody.**

The surface is Greek‑village farce: the disgraced TV chef and the goat incident, the bouzouki
busker whose instrument eats a pack slot, the cruise‑ship coachloads, the corporate team‑lead
who wants to scramble up an unmarked cliff "because we're on a schedule." The substrate is
the genuine ache of *or no Life* — you're back in your mother's kitchen because nothing else
worked out, and the Hilux is the first freedom you have bought yourself in a decade.

**The comedy is not decoration on top of the sadness. The comedy is what earns the sadness.**
A game that is sad for two hours is a chore; a game that is funny for two hours and then,
once, quietly devastating, is unforgettable.

---

## 2. The ratio rule (with the parody lift)

Roughly: **comedy is the default register; pathos is rationed and always earned.** Most beats
are warm, dry, or outright funny. Many are happy to push into outright parody — the over‑
equipped K2 client in the olive groves, the moped that does not, in fact, have a working horn,
the cousin who "knows a guy."

**The pathos is allowed to surface at four spots**, and basically only at those:
1. **Phase transitions** — the moment Maria gets a radio, the morning after the Hilux lands.
2. **When a named guide's arc pays off or breaks** — they leave, they shine, they quit.
3. **When the trail‑days drift becomes visible** — the protagonist used to lace their own
   boots and now assigns. The reckoning of that.
4. **The ending.**

When the ache *does* land, the passage carries no jokes. We do it cleanly and then get out —
the contrast is the whole effect. Never milk it.

**Parody is the new permission.** The original PHASE‑ARCHITECTURE §6 doctrine kept comedy
"warm and dry"; playtest has shown the game can lean further. The over‑equipped client, the
mother callbacks, the Alani who knows a guy whose merchandise is "of uncertain provenance" —
these are allowed to be properly funny, not just gently amused. Push the line. Pull back at
the four pathos spots above and only there.

## 3. Voice rules

### Person & tense
- **Second person, always.** *"You hand over spare bottles. A crisis solved before it started."*
  The player is the guide; the game narrates their day back to them.
- **Present tense for the world.** *"The wind is up off the gulf, snapping at the ridgelines."*
- **Past tense for resolved actions.** *"You wait while they stock up."*
- **Never "I" / "we".** The game never addresses itself. No fourth wall.

### Register
- **Literary, terse, complete sentences.** No telegraphic UI fragments (*"Water +2"*). Numbers
  go in the instruments strip; everything else is woven into prose.
- **Quiet authority** is the baseline emotional pitch. The narrator has seen this before. The
  narrator is not surprised by what the family did at the trailhead.
- **Dry over wet.** When in doubt cut a clause, drop an adverb, let the image do the work.
- **Greek specificity** is a comedy engine and a credibility engine at once — real places,
  real textures (cicadas, thyme, the bruise‑coloured storm horizon, the kiosk, the taverna).
- **Verdicts, not metrics.** *"A good day's work."* / *"The clients left glowing."* /
  *"A complaint or two came back."* The mechanics know the score; the prose tells the truth.

### Humour
- Comes from **character and situation**, never from winking at the player.
- The game is **never sarcastic about itself.** No "oh great, another booking" energy.
- It IS allowed to be self‑deprecating about the *player‑character* ("a small chance any
  buy is dodgy", "you push the schedule and the schedule pushes back").
- **Don't explain the joke.** If the line lands, leave it. If it doesn't, cut it.

### People in the prose
- **Guides are named individuals**: Maria, Nikos, Alexios, Thalia, Petros. They get **specs**:
  *"sure‑footed on scree"*, *"calm in foul weather"*, *"wonderful with families"*.
- **Clients are described in noun phrases, not types**: *"a family with two young children"*,
  *"a coachload of cruise tourists"*, *"three photographers chasing the light"*. Never
  "Client (Family)" or "Tourists × 6".
- **Recurring villagers carry names**: Kyría Voúla and her blood‑pressure diary, Cousin
  Mitsos in the yard with the spanner, Stelios's brother who brought the gear, Kostas's boy
  in Stoúpa who bought a car. Each can come back across seasons.

## 4. Greek diction

Lean on Greek words and place names where they sit naturally. Don't translate or italic‑
explain — the player learns by context. Accents matter (`í`, `á`, `ó`, `é`) — copy them
faithfully.

### People & address
- **paidí mou** — neutral diminutive ("my child"). Used in the kitchen narration; never in
  hero‑specific lines now that the heroes are fixed‑gender.
- **yie mou** / **yióka mou** — "my son" (for ♂ heroes; mum's pitch line uses *yie mou*).
- **kóri mou** / **korítsi mou** — "my daughter" (for ♀ heroes).
- **Kyría / Kýrios** — Mrs / Mr (formal). Always written with the accent: *Kyría Voúla*.
- **Yiayiá** — grandmother. Lower‑case in narration unless naming.

### Food & evening
- **gemistá** — stuffed peppers and tomatoes (dish option 1).
- **pastítsio** — baked pasta with béchamel and meat (dish option 2).
- **bakaliáros & skordaliá** — fried cod and garlic dip (dish option 3).
- **youvétsi** — Sunday lamb and orzo (dish option 4).
- **panigíri** — the village festival in August. Recurs across seasons.
- **kafeneío** — village coffee house, the social spine. (Spelling: prefer *kafeneío* over
  the simpler *kafenion*.)
- **tsípouro** / **ouzo** — what your uncle presses into your hand at the panigíri.
- **bouzouki / bouzoukia** — the instrument and the bars where it is played. The Bouzouki
  Busker hero carries one.
- **souvláki** — what the engineer mentally estimates the throughput of.
- **kombolói** — worry beads. Hinted at the Greek villager's collar.

### Weather & sea
- **meltémi** — the killer northwest summer wind. The signature terror for sea kayak. Use
  the proper spelling with the accent when the wind is named diegetically.
- **sirocco / sahara haze** — the southerly that brings red dust. Out of scope for now but
  reserved.

### Places
Real and accurate. Accents preserved where standard:
- **Kalamatá / Kalamata** — the home base. The kitchen is in Kalamata.
- **Polylímnio** — the waterfall pools. (Spec uses *Polylimnio* without accent — keep
  consistent with current ROUTES.)
- **Voidokiliá / Voidokilia**, **Gialova**, **Pylos**, **Methóni / Methoni**, **Koróni**,
  **Finikoúnda**, **Mavromáti**, **Mystrás / Mystras**, **Lousios**, **Neda**, **Taygetos**,
  **Cape Tainaron / Tenaro**, **Stoúpa** (Kostas's village), **Kardamýli / Kardamyli**.
- **Mani** — the peninsula. Capitalised, no accent.

### Stylings that signal Greek without translating
- "She tells the neighbours you have **many friends**." (idiom that travels)
- "**You know what you should do.**" (mum's setup)
- "**Three years.** *Three years.*" (mum's reiteration into the icon)
- "Kostas's boy does it, up in Stoúpa. **He bought a car.**" (the neighbourly comparison)

## 5. Typography & punctuation

- **Sentence case** in narrative. *"Begin a season"*, *"Build your guide"*, *"The winter
  office"*.
- **ALL CAPS, wide‑tracked monospace (Spline Sans Mono)** for *instrument* labels:
  `DAY · CASH · REPUTE · ENERGY · FORECAST`. These are the dials; they read as laser‑etched
  into brass. Implemented via the existing `.lab` / `.day-tag` classes.
- **Em dash (`—`)**, never `--`. The em dash is the workhorse — both for narrative pauses
  (*"Mountain Leader — the hardest summits"*) and for kicker punctuation.
- **Middle dot (`·`)** separates atoms in instrument lines: `Day 4 · Forecast` /
  `Year 1 · April 5`.
- **Curly quotes** (`'` `'` `"` `"`), never straight (`'` `"`). Watch apostrophes inside JS
  strings — *"today's"* once broke a build. See `CLAUDE.md` gotchas.
- **Currency: `€` prefix, no space, no decimals.** `€1500`, never `€ 1500` or `1500 €` or
  `€1500.00`.
- **Negatives use the Unicode minus sign `−`, not the hyphen `-`.** `−€60`, `−5 rep`. The
  minus is heavier and lines up vertically with the plus.
- **Dingbats** are the only glyphs used as icons. Sanctioned set (do not add others):
  `▸ ▶ ✓ ✦ ▦ ☼ ❧ ⌂ ✕ ≡ · — ☾ ⚒ ◈ ✂ +`. No emoji. Ever.
- **Ellipses** are three dots, no Unicode `…`. Use sparingly — let the period do the work
  most of the time.
- **Lists in prose** are spelled out: *"water, snacks, a borrowed bottle of suncream"* — not
  bulleted UI lists.

## 6. Locked decisions

These came out of playtest and naming sessions. Treat them as canonical.

- **Game title**: *A Guide's Life or no Life.* Two lines on the hero — *A Guide's Life*
  in serif, *or no Life* in italic gold underneath.
- **Kicker** (the small all‑caps line above the hero): *A career in the outdoors, money, and
  minor catastrophes.*
- **Tagline used in the title screen verdict**: *"You graduated from your guide course because
  nothing else worked out. The Peloponnese is now your problem."*
- **The slacker register is the META voice** — title, transitions, end‑of‑run screens, menu
  labels, mum's lines. *Inside* a trip the prose stays more literary; the meta‑voice/in‑trip
  contrast sharpens both.
- **Hero diminutives are fixed per hero.** Each of the ten heroes (HEROES.md) has a locked
  gender, so mum's diminutive is always the right one — *yie mou* for the medic, *kóri mou*
  for the influencer, etc. No gender toggle anywhere.
- **The kitchen is the opening scene** — not a profile screen, not a stats picker. Beat 0
  (cold open + name) → Beat 1 (dish) → Beat 2 (hero) → Beat 3 (mum's pitch) → Beat 4
  (panigíri setup with discipline, loan, kit).
- **The Hometown is Kalamata**, for everyone, for now. Anchored by the kitchen.
- **Mum is a recurring presence**, not a one‑off. She callbacks at the Hilux purchase
  ("It runs," she says, to the pot. "That is enough.") and at first hire. Future callbacks
  at season report, retirement, etc. — written sparingly, never milked.

## 7. Worked examples

### Tuning a line up the comedy register

**Reverent** (avoid — drains the room):
> You wait while phones come out and shoulders relax. This is why they came.

**Comic‑heightened** (the baseline target):
> You wait. Out come the phones. A German couple weeps, gently, at a goat. This — the weeping,
> the goat, the fact that you get paid for it — is why you came. You think.

The "You think." is the ache, rationed and earned, landing on a laugh.

**Parody‑pushed** (acceptable for non‑pathos beats — the new permission):
> You wait. Out come the phones. A German couple weeps, gently, at a goat. Behind them an
> Englishman is filming the weeping for a podcast called *Things Greece Has Taught Me*. The
> goat has had enough and walks off. The podcast does not, you can already tell, deserve it.

### Mum's voice — narrow band, recognisable

> "Three years of medical school," she tells the icon over the door, not you. "*Three years.*"
> You don't correct her to four sittings of the same anatomy exam.

> "Kostas's boy does it, up in Stoúpa. He bought a car." A pause; the knife goes in clean.

> "It runs," she says, to the pot. "That is enough."

The shape: she addresses an object, not you. She repeats. She is busy with food. The verdict
arrives obliquely.

### Meta‑voice (slacker) vs in‑trip voice (literary)

**Meta**, title screen:
> A career in the outdoors, money, and minor catastrophes.

**Meta**, hire scene:
> You can keep doing this, or you can stop being the only one.

**In‑trip**, weather turns:
> You set off in clear. The day has had other ideas. Cloud spills over the ridge and the
> wind grows teeth.

Both register as the same game. Neither tries to do the other's job.

### Greek seasoning, done lightly

**Wrong** (heavy translation):
> Your mother (in Greek, "mána") prepared gemistá (stuffed vegetables) for dinner.

**Right** (Greek does the work):
> She has made gemistá — and she will not hear that you have a different favourite now.

## 8. Anti‑patterns

Things that have been tried elsewhere and would visibly clash here. Do not write these:

- **Emojis.** Ever. Not for warmth, not for clarity, not for "fun."
- **Telegraphic UI fragments** — *"Water acquired!"*, *"Rep +2"*, *"Quest complete!"*,
  *"Loot: 1 × Rope"*. The instruments strip handles numbers; the prose handles meaning.
- **Sarcasm about the game itself** — *"oh great, ANOTHER booking"*, *"buckle up, you're
  doing the kayak intro again"*. The game does not address itself.
- **Generic adventure‑game language** — *XP*, *quest*, *level*, *loot*, *boss*, *unlocked!*,
  *achievement*, *gold pieces*. Outdoor‑guide career is the frame; words from that world only.
- **American breeziness or corporate cheer** — *"Awesome! Let's go!"*, *"You crushed it!"*,
  *"Way to go!"*. Quiet authority is the default; warmth where deserved.
- **Pure misery, unrelieved** — see §2. If a passage hurts, the next passage breathes.
- **Over‑explaining a joke** — the second sentence killing the first. Let lines land or don't.
- **Wikipedia voice on Greek words** — *"gemistá (γεμιστά), literally 'filled'"* with the
  etymology. Use the word; let context teach.
- **Inconsistent currency** — `1500€`, `1500 EUR`, `EUR 1500`, `€1,500.00`. Only `€1500`.
- **Straight quotes / hyphens for em‑dashes / regular minus signs**. See §5.

---

## 9. When in doubt

Read the line aloud. If it sounds like a tour brochure, cut it. If it sounds like a marketing
agency, cut it. If it sounds like a tutorial popup, cut it. If it sounds like *something a
person who has actually run a guiding company in Messinia might say while shaking their head
at a story*, keep it.

**The compass**: warm, written, Peloponnese. Comic by default. Parody where the line earns it.
Quiet when the ache lands.
